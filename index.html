Hydrogen Fuel Cell Powered Toy Car

The goal of the fuel cell car project was to build a car and use a hydrogen fuel cell to power the motor driver. As an added challenge the car with the best run time and fuel economy got the highest mark. The average run time for the car within the class was just under six and a half minutes; I managed to get my car ran for just over nineteen minutes.

That large difference in runtime wasn’t just luck (I hope); I‘d spent a lot of time researching pressure vessels and gas transfer to understand how to optimize fuel economy. During week one of the project I began by drafting several solid models of the cars body and gas containment vessels. I’d built and machined several prototypes and tested them all for transfer efficiency percentage. Once the physical design was confirmed I began preforming chemical calculations to determine how much hydrogen fuel was being created by the electrolysis reaction. Once the stoichiometric and reaction calculations were completed and confirmed, I began designing the PCB controller that would allow me to remotely control the car.

The controller used was a PS3 controller relayed to an Arduino Uno via a Bluetooth dongle. The servo motor was used to turn a 360 degree lateral platform with two wheels to a stepping angle of 5 degrees. Once the car was finished it was able to navigate several tightly made mazes with considerable speed and accuracy. It was really great being able to integrate something I’d learned outside of class into a project in class. I see a lot of cool things on the way.

The Sumo Robot was my first experience with a competitive robotic build. The goal of this project was to design and build a small 10”x10”x10” robot that would push its competition out of the ring.

The robots were placed inside a black ring with a white pinstripe surrounding it. Both robots were placed parallel to each other facing in opposite directions. They were then simultaneously turned on.

From here the robots would begin spinning until their infrared sensors picked up the signal bouncing off of the opposing robot. The bot would then proceed to push the opposing robot out of the ring. If the bot got to the edge of the ring a system similar to the line follower robot would detect the line and steer the bot back to safety.

Fuel Cell Car

Hydrogen Fuel-celled Toy Car

The goal of the hydrogen fuel-cell car was to build a car powered by hydrogen fuel-cells. The additional factor to this project was to create a line-following algorithm, so that the fastest team that completed the line-mze tracks got the highest mark.

This project was a mix of three core courses, CHE102 (Chemistry), MTE100 (Mechatronics Engineering), GENE121 (C++). Our team had to research the pressure vessels associated with each fuel-cell, as well as the gas transfer to maximize the run-time. Once the research was done, we performed various chemical calculations to determine how much hydrogen fuel was created. As for the programming, we were given a designated IDE to successfully code the line-following algorithm. Bascially, the algorithm would allow the robot to follow the line-maze around the tracks and get to the endpoints without interffering with the obstacles.

This was my very first group project with my cohorts, and it was really interesting how I was able to integrate core courses to build a robot that runs on hydrogen-cells and navigate itself on line-maze.
